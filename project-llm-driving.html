Overview

As SAE Level 3 automated driving systems enter the market, drivers face a new challenge: understanding when and how to intervene when the vehicle requests control. Existing training approaches — static manuals, generic videos — fail to account for individual differences in prior knowledge and trust calibration.

This study designed and evaluated an AI-powered personalized training system that adapts feedback to each driver's knowledge level and automation trust profile, grounding all content in validated Level 3 automation materials via Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG).

Problem

SAE Level 3 systems require drivers to resume control on demand — a cognitively demanding task with real safety implications. Yet most driver education treats automation as a single, uniform topic, ignoring individual differences in:

  • Prior knowledge of automation capabilities and limitations
  • Calibration of trust (over-trust vs. under-trust)
  • Preferred learning modality

Methodology

A between-subjects experiment (N=80) compared three training conditions:

  • Interactive video-based training — structured multimedia content
  • Simulator-based training — hands-on practice in a driving simulator
  • AI-personalized training — LLM-driven feedback tailored to individual knowledge and trust levels

Prior to training, all participants completed a knowledge pre-test and an automation trust scale to establish their baseline profile. This data fed directly into the AI system's personalization logic.

System Design

The AI training system was built on a RAG-informed LLM workflow. Validated Level 3 automation reference materials were indexed and retrieved dynamically based on each participant's knowledge gaps — ensuring feedback was both personalized and grounded in accurate content.

Design Principle

Personalization was driven by pre-assessed knowledge level and trust calibration scores — not arbitrary preference settings — ensuring the system adapted to demonstrated needs rather than self-reported ones.

Recommendations

Based on the study findings, the following recommendations were developed for Level 3 driver training program design:

  • Incorporate pre-training knowledge and trust assessments to tailor content delivery
  • Use RAG-based retrieval to ground AI-generated feedback in validated, regulation-aligned materials
  • Design training that targets both knowledge gaps and trust miscalibration simultaneously
  • Evaluate effectiveness across diverse driver populations, including older adults and novice drivers
