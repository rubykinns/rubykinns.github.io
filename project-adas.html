Overview

Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) — including adaptive cruise control, lane-keeping assist, and automatic emergency braking — are rapidly becoming standard in modern vehicles. Yet drivers frequently misuse or over-rely on these systems due to poor mental models of their capabilities and limitations.

Sponsored by the AAA Foundation for Traffic Safety, this study investigated how different driver training interventions affect mental model accuracy, trust calibration, decision-making, and real-world driving performance with SAE Level 1 and Level 2 ADAS.

Problem

ADAS misuse is a leading contributor to technology-related crashes. Drivers who over-trust automation disengage from the driving task; those who under-trust it fail to use safety features that could protect them. Both failure modes stem from inaccurate mental models — and both are preventable with effective training.

  • What types of training content most effectively improve mental model accuracy for ADAS?
  • Do training improvements transfer to real-world decision-making and driving performance?
  • How does training affect trust calibration — reducing both over-trust and under-trust?

Methodology

A mixed-methods behavioral study was designed to capture the full picture of driver-ADAS interaction across cognitive, attentional, and behavioral dimensions. Three data collection modalities were integrated:

  • Driving simulator experimentation — standardized ADAS scenarios enabling controlled measurement of driving performance and decision-making
  • Eye tracking data capture — continuous gaze recording to assess attentional allocation and monitoring behavior during ADAS-assisted driving
  • Survey-based observation — validated scales measuring mental model accuracy, trust, and perceived usability before and after training

Measures

Four primary outcomes were measured to evaluate the effect of training interventions:

  • Mental model accuracy — knowledge test assessing understanding of ADAS capabilities, limitations, and appropriate use cases
  • Trust calibration — validated automation trust scale measuring alignment between driver trust and actual system reliability
  • Decision-making — scenario-based judgment tasks evaluating appropriate ADAS engagement and disengagement decisions
  • Driving performance — simulator metrics including lane deviation, reaction time, and safety-critical event response
Methodological Strength

The combination of simulator performance data, eye tracking, and self-report measures enabled triangulation across behavioral, attentional, and cognitive dimensions — providing a more complete picture of training effects than any single method alone.

Key Findings

Specific training interventions significantly improved mental model accuracy, decision-making quality, and driving performance with ADAS. Training effects were measurable and consistent across all four outcome dimensions.

Key Result

Training content that explicitly addressed ADAS limitations — not just capabilities — produced the greatest improvements in both mental model accuracy and trust calibration, reducing both over-reliance and under-use of ADAS features.

Recommendations

Strategic recommendations were synthesized and delivered to AAA Foundation stakeholders for integration into driver education programs:

  • Include explicit instruction on ADAS limitations and failure modes — not just features and benefits
  • Use scenario-based decision tasks during training to build appropriate trust calibration
  • Incorporate hands-on simulator practice alongside conceptual instruction for maximum transfer
  • Target monitoring behavior explicitly — training drivers to maintain appropriate visual attention even during ADAS-assisted driving
  • Tailor training depth to driver experience level and prior ADAS exposure
