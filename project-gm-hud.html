Overview

Head-Up Displays (HUDs) are increasingly used in modern vehicles to deliver safety-critical warnings without requiring drivers to look away from the road. However, poor HUD design can lead to miscomprehension or delayed response — with serious safety consequences.

In direct partnership with General Motors, this large-scale human factors study evaluated 33 HUD interface variants across 1,500 participants, identifying which design elements most reliably produce fast, accurate driver responses to warning signals.

Problem

General Motors needed empirical evidence to guide HUD warning design decisions at scale. Key unanswered questions included:

  • Which color combinations maximize comprehension accuracy under real driving conditions?
  • How does warning timing affect driver response latency?
  • Does icon/text size produce meaningful differences in recognition speed?
  • Which specific design combinations perform best across diverse driver populations?

Methodology

The study was conducted via Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk), enabling rapid recruitment of a diverse, large-scale participant sample. Each participant was exposed to a subset of HUD interface stimuli and asked to respond to warning signals under simulated driving conditions.

Custom behavioral data capture tools were developed in Python and JavaScript to ensure standardized, reproducible measurement of response time and comprehension accuracy across all 1,500 participants.

Design Variables

Three primary HUD design variables were systematically manipulated across 33 interface variants:

  • Color — hue, contrast, and luminance combinations for warning signals
  • Timing — onset duration, flash frequency, and persistence of warning displays
  • Size — icon scale, text size, and display footprint within the HUD field
Design Approach

All 33 variants were derived from a systematic factorial combination of the three variables, enabling direct comparison of individual and interactive effects on comprehension and response time.

Key Findings

Specific interface designs yielded significantly higher comprehension accuracy and faster response times than others. The results revealed clear interaction effects between color and timing variables.

Key Result

Specific HUD design configurations produced measurably superior comprehension accuracy and response time. These optimal designs were directly delivered as engineering recommendations to General Motors at the executive level.

Recommendations

Optimal design recommendations were delivered directly to General Motors engineering and executive teams, informing production-level HUD design decisions:

  • Prioritize high-contrast color combinations with sufficient luminance differentiation from ambient light
  • Optimize warning onset timing to allow driver recognition without creating startle responses
  • Ensure minimum icon/text sizes meet legibility thresholds across diverse driver age groups
  • Avoid design combinations that showed confound effects between color and timing variables
