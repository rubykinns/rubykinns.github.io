Overview

Cabby is a comprehensive UX design system for self-driving taxis — designing the full passenger experience across three distinct touchpoints: a mobile booking app, an in-car interactive display, and an exterior LED communication panel for pedestrians.

As autonomous vehicles transition from novelty to daily infrastructure, riders face a fundamental new challenge: trusting and interacting with a vehicle that has no driver. Cabby was designed to bridge that trust gap through clear information architecture, proactive communication, and personalized ride experiences.

Background

01 · Background

The shift from human-driven to driverless taxis represents a fundamental paradigm change — not just in technology, but in how people communicate with their transportation.

In traditional taxis, passengers rely on gesture, voice, and eye contact with the driver to communicate needs, signal discomfort, or request changes. Without a driver, none of these channels exist.

"An IoT device that helps self-driving taxis communicate with pedestrians and passengers inside and outside."

User Flow

04 · User Flow

Five end-to-end journey stages were mapped across all three touchpoints, covering the complete passenger experience from booking through post-ride rating.

Lo-fi Prototype

05 · Lo-fi

Paper prototyping sessions allowed the team to rapidly explore interaction patterns and layout concepts before committing to digital wireframes. Handwritten screens were used to test core flows and identify ambiguities early.

Lo-fi digital wireframes followed for both the mobile app and the in-car systems, covering all screens across the 5 journey stages.

User Testing

06 · User Testing

User testing sessions were conducted with participants interacting with the lo-fi prototypes to evaluate comprehension, navigation clarity, and the intuitiveness of key interaction patterns — particularly around the unlock flow and emergency stop.

Key Finding from Testing

Users consistently struggled to locate their Cabby upon arrival — particularly in crowded pickup areas. This led directly to the custom ID system and AR navigation feature in the hi-fi design.

Wireframe

07 · Wireframe

Mid-fidelity wireframes were developed in Figma for both the mobile app and the in-car display systems, incorporating user testing feedback and refining the information hierarchy.

Car Modelling

08 · Car Modelling

To fully realize the Cabby experience — particularly the exterior LED display and door touch screen — the vehicle itself needed to be designed. A 3D car model was created from scratch, including technical blueprints across all views and a fully rendered 3D model.

The vehicle concept includes detection displays on front and rear for pedestrian communication, a door-mounted touch screen for keyless unlock, a sunroof, and four sensor units on the roof. The exterior ID display was designed to be fully customizable by the passenger.

Hi-fi Design · Mobile App

09 · Hi-fi · Mobile

The high-fidelity mobile app applied the Cabby visual system — dark navy backgrounds, teal accents, and clean typography — to the complete booking and ride experience.

Hi-fi Design · Door Touch Screen

10 · Hi-fi · Door Screen

The door-mounted touch screen provides a physical fallback for unlocking — offering passcode entry and security question recovery when the mobile app isn't available. The flow maps cleanly from enter → confirm → error → help → unlocked.

Hi-fi Design · Exterior LED Display

11 · Hi-fi · Exterior LED

A core innovation of Cabby is the exterior LED communication system — addressing one of the most pressing unsolved problems in autonomous vehicle design: how does a driverless car communicate its intent to pedestrians?

Design Rationale

The PASS / STOP / ID signal system was designed to be legible at a glance, without language dependency — ensuring the vehicle can communicate intent across languages, literacy levels, and visibility conditions.

Hi-fi Design · Internal Display

12 · Hi-fi · Internal Display

The in-car dashboard was designed as a rich ambient information layer — giving passengers a sense of control and situational awareness without requiring active interaction. Key screens include the main dashboard, voice assistant, emergency stop, and pedestrian detection overlay.

The main dashboard surfaces:

  • Real-time speed and speed limit
  • Live route map with current location and ETA
  • Music controls with Now Playing
  • Driving mode controls (Park / Drive / Reverse)
  • Ambient vehicle status — AC, lights, fan, temperature
  • Emergency stop request button (persistent, always accessible)

Reflection

Cabby was one of my earliest end-to-end UX design projects, and it directly shaped the research questions I pursued throughout my PhD. Several lessons from this project became foundational to my later work:

  • Trust is a design material. Every touchpoint in Cabby — the LED signal, the voice assistant, the real-time dashboard — was ultimately a trust mechanism. This framing became central to my later human factors research on driver trust in ADAS and SAE Level 3 automation.
  • Multi-touchpoint design requires system thinking. Mobile, in-car display, and exterior LED had to feel like one coherent product, not three parallel designs. Designing these surfaces together forced rigorous thinking about information hierarchy and handoff.
  • Car modelling deepened the design. Building the 3D vehicle model wasn't decorative — it forced precise decisions about where sensors, displays, and access points would physically live, making the UX constraints real rather than abstract.
  • Research and design reinforce each other. The needfinding phase made every design decision more defensible. And conversely, designing Cabby gave me intuitions about autonomous vehicle UX that later informed how I designed and analyzed my PhD research studies.
