Overview

Construction sites are increasingly integrating robotic systems — from autonomous excavators to collaborative assembly robots. Workers must learn to operate alongside these systems safely and effectively, yet existing training programs rarely address the unique demands of human-robot collaboration (HRC) in construction environments.

This generative research project identified training gaps and user needs through in-depth expert interviews, then translated findings into VR-based training design requirements and Figma prototypes.

Problem

As construction robotics advances, the industry faces a critical skills gap: workers lack structured training for safe, effective collaboration with robotic systems. Current training approaches are either too generic or do not exist at all for emerging HRC scenarios.

  • What specific knowledge and skills do workers need for effective human-robot collaboration on construction sites?
  • What gaps exist in current training programs?
  • How can immersive VR address these gaps in ways that physical training cannot?

Methodology

Semi-structured interviews were conducted with 8 industry experts and university faculty with direct experience in construction robotics, workforce training, and human-robot interaction. Participants included:

  • Construction site managers with robotics deployment experience
  • University faculty specializing in construction engineering and HRC research
  • VR training developers with construction industry clients

Each interview was approximately 60–90 minutes, yielding 120 minutes of analyzed interview data. All interviews were recorded, transcribed, and imported into NVivo 14 for systematic analysis.

Qualitative Analysis

A 9-dimension qualitative codebook was developed to systematically capture training needs, HRC challenges, and VR design requirements across all interview data.

Rigor & Reliability

Inter-rater reliability was formally established before full-scale coding — ensuring that the 881 coded segments across 120 minutes of interview data reflect consistent, replicable interpretation rather than a single coder's perspective.

Pattern finding and thematic summarization across the coded segments generated a consolidated set of training requirements and design priorities for the VR training interface.

Prototype Development

Research findings were translated directly into design artifacts — Figma prototypes for a VR-based construction robot training interface. Prototypes were developed at two fidelity levels and tested on two platforms:

  • Desktop version — lower barrier to access, suitable for pre-site onboarding
  • Oculus Quest 2 version — immersive, spatially accurate simulation of construction environments

Key Findings

Expert interviews revealed consistent patterns in training gaps and VR design needs across industry and academic perspectives.

Key Insight

Experts consistently identified situational awareness as the most critical and undertrained skill for human-robot collaboration — specifically, the ability to predict robot movement trajectories and respond appropriately in shared physical spaces.

Design Recommendations

Findings were synthesized into VR training design requirements for human-robot collaboration in construction:

  • Simulate robot trajectory visualization to build spatial awareness and safe proximity habits
  • Include scenario-based training for edge cases — unexpected robot behavior, emergency stops, sensor failures
  • Design progressive skill-building sequences from low-stakes to high-complexity HRC tasks
  • Embed procedural knowledge checks at key decision points within VR training scenarios
  • Support cross-platform delivery (desktop + VR headset) to maximize accessibility across worker demographics
