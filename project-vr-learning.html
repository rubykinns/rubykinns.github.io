Overview

As spatial computing and immersive VR environments enter educational settings, instructors face entirely new challenges: how to structure a classroom, engage students, and deliver content effectively when the learning space is virtual and three-dimensional.

This project took an instructor-centered perspective — investigating how environmental settings, design parameters, and VR teaching tools shape the instructor's experience in immersive learning environments, with the goal of generating actionable design requirements.

Problem

Most VR classroom research focuses on student experience — learner engagement, knowledge retention, presence. The instructor perspective has been largely overlooked, despite instructors being the primary agents who design and deliver educational experiences.

  • What classroom environment elements feel natural and effective for instructors in VR?
  • Which teaching tools translate well from physical to virtual classrooms?
  • What entirely new features does VR enable that physical spaces cannot?

Methodology

A qualitative approach was used, combining focus group interviews, co-design workshops, qualitative coding, and pattern analysis to capture both instructors' lived experience and VR developers' technical perspectives.

  • Focus group interviews with instructors — current challenges and needs in VR teaching
  • Co-design workshops — participatory sessions with instructors and VR designers/developers
  • Qualitative coding — pattern finding and thematic summarization across participant inputs

Co-design Process

Co-design workshops were structured to bring instructors and VR designers into a shared creative process. Participants collaboratively identified and prioritized:

  • Essential classroom environmental elements (spatial layout, lighting, audio zones)
  • Educational features needed for effective VR instruction
  • Prototype concepts for new VR-native teaching interactions
Design Principle

Co-design ensured that VR classroom features were grounded in actual instructor workflows rather than assumptions about how teaching should translate to virtual space.

Key Findings

Qualitative analysis surfaced consistent patterns across instructor and developer perspectives regarding what makes a VR learning environment effective.

Key Insight

Instructors prioritized spatial control and student visibility above all other features — the ability to see all students simultaneously and move between them fluidly was identified as the most critical VR classroom affordance.

Design Recommendations

Findings were synthesized into design requirements for effective VR learning environments:

  • Provide instructors with a persistent spatial overview of the full virtual classroom
  • Enable flexible environmental configuration (seating arrangements, zones) without technical expertise
  • Design VR-native teaching tools that go beyond physical whiteboard equivalents
  • Reduce cognitive overhead for instructors by automating routine classroom management tasks
  • Support social presence cues that signal student attention and engagement
